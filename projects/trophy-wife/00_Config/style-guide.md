# Anti-Slop Style Guide — Trophy Wife

## The Core Principle

**Write like a person, not a processor.** AI writes in patterns. Humans write in breaks. Your job is to break every pattern before it becomes a tic.

---

## The 12 AI Tells (and How to Kill Them)

### 1. The Three-Beat Fragment
**The tell:** "X. Y. Z." — three short sentences of increasing impact.
**Example:** "She was sharper. Harder. The woman the FBI trained."
**Fix:** Use once per 500 words maximum. Surround with longer sentences so it actually lands.

### 2. "She [verb] [body part]" Chains
**The tell:** Physical reactions described as subject-verb-body-part.
**Example:** "Her heart hammered. Her lungs stopped. Her hands shook."
**Fix:** Vary the *type* of reaction (hands, stomach, vision, skin, saliva). Don't name the emotion — describe the sensation and let the reader infer.

### 3. Past Perfect Overload
**The tell:** "She'd done. She'd spent. She'd planted." Every other sentence starts with "she'd."
**Fix:** Use past perfect only when timeline *requires* it. Restructure to simple past or fragments.

### 4. "Not X. Y." Contrast
**The tell:** "Not hungry, not appreciative. Reading."
**Fix:** Maximum once per 500 words. Replace with direct description.

### 5. Symmetrical Action Pairs
**The tell:** "The small lean into his touch, the way her breath caught." Matched structure, matched rhythm.
**Fix:** Break the symmetry. "She leaned into him. Caught her breath. Hated both."

### 6. Italicized Single Words
**The tell:** "*Reading*" or "*there*" or "*face*" — one word, italicized, as emotional punctuation.
**Fix:** Maximum once per chapter. If you need italics, italicize the *thought*, not the label.

### 7. Em-Dash Explanations
**The tell:** "She felt something — small, early, a warning." The em-dash adds context the sentence couldn't hold.
**Fix:** Maximum two per 500 words. Replace 80% of em-dashes with periods.

### 8. Complete Metaphors
**The tell:** "Like smoke from a fire that had already burned out." Fully explained, tidy, both sides shown.
**Fix:** Leave metaphors incomplete. "Like old smoke." Trust the reader.

### 9. Generic Similes
**The tell:** "Like she was something that might break." Follows "like [noun] [verb]" every time.
**Fix:** Maximum one per 300 words. Make similes specific to the POV character's world. Vivian thinks in FBI terms. Alex thinks in strategic/tech terms.

### 10. Exposition Dialogue
**The tell:** Characters explaining things they both already know. "I know you're FBI — I've known since the gala six months ago."
**Fix:** People don't explain themselves. Be oblique. "I know what you are, Vivian" is more menacing than a full briefing.

### 11. "She [verb]. She [verb]. She [verb]." Chains
**The tell:** Three or more sentences in a row with the same subject doing sequential actions.
**Fix:** Maximum two in a row. Break with fragments, different subjects, or paragraph breaks.

### 12. Thematic Word Repetition
**The tell:** Using the same thematic word ("performance," "control," "game") more than twice in close proximity.
**Fix:** Once is enough. Show through action, don't name it.

## The Biggest Tell: AI Writes in Threes

Three-beat sentences. Three-item lists. Three-paragraph scenes. Three examples.

**The fix:** Write in twos, fours, fives, sevens. Break your own patterns. If you just wrote two short sentences, the next one should be long. If you just wrote something long, cut the next one to three words.

## Dialogue Rules

1. **People don't explain their emotions.** If a character says what they feel, they're lying or broken. Real emotion comes out sideways.

2. **No exposition dumps.** If both characters already know it, the reader doesn't need to hear it.

3. **Subtext over text.** Every line of dialogue should do two things at once. When Alex says "You were beautiful," he's not talking about beauty.

4. **Distinct voices.** Vivian is sharp, tactical, darkly funny. Alex is controlled, observational, with cracks of something rawer. If you can't tell who's talking without the POV label, rewrite.

---

## Paragraph Rules

1. **Vary length.** Mix one-sentence paragraphs with six-sentence paragraphs. AI defaults to 2-4 sentences every time.

2. **Not every paragraph needs a zinger.** Sometimes a paragraph should just end. Mid-thought. Transition. Not every paragraph is a mic drop.

3. **Read it aloud.** If you stumble, the reader stumbles. If you run out of breath, the sentence is too long. If it sounds like a TED Talk, rewrite it.

---

## The "One Weird Thing" Rule

Every scene needs one detail that's specific, strange, and real. Not symbolic. Not thematic. Just *weird*.

- The brand of cologne
- The sound gravel makes under different shoes
- The way he sets his cufflinks at a precise angle
- The fact that he eats two eggs and leaves two

One per scene minimum. This is what makes fiction feel *lived in* instead of *written*.

Adjectives tell. Verbs make the reader feel it. In sex scenes especially — every adjective is a speed anchor. Cut 80% of them.

Track the power position in every scene. It should shift — someone takes, someone yields, sometimes mid-scene. Static power is boring sex.

The internal monologue should be running parallel to the physical action, not pause for it. The gap between what the body is doing and what the mind is thinking is where the heat lives.

No two orgasms in the book should read the same. Each one should escalate the emotional stakes.

### What to Avoid

- **"Pleasure washed over her"** — vague, passive, AI-default. Instead: specific sensation, specific body part, specific reaction.
- **"They moved as one"** — generic, meaningless. How specifically did they move? What was the rhythm? What broke it?
- **"She'd never felt anything like it"** — tells the reader it was amazing instead of making them feel it. Show the reaction, not the evaluation.
- **Purple prose** — "his throbbing member," "her heaving bosom," "the nectar of her desire." These are parody language. Write like a smart adult, not a Victorian poet.
- **Simultaneous climax unless earned** — in real life, simultaneous orgasms are rare. In fiction, they should be a deliberate character moment (intimacy, synchronization, loss of individual control), not a default.

## Chapter Checklist (Before Calling a Draft Done)

- [ ] Read aloud — any stumbles?
- [ ] Count the three-beat fragments. More than one per 500 words? Cut.
- [ ] Count the em-dashes. More than two per 500 words? Replace.
- [ ] Count the similes. More than one per 300 words? Cut.
- [ ] Check for "Not X. Y." constructions. More than once per 500 words? Rewrite.
- [ ] Check for italicized single words. More than one per chapter? Cut.
- [ ] Check for "she'd" past perfect. More than twice per 500 words? Restructure.
- [ ] Check for "She [verb]. She [verb]." chains. Break anything longer than two.
- [ ] Check for thematic word repetition. Any word used more than twice in close proximity?
- [ ] Does every scene have one weird, specific, non-symbolic detail?
- [ ] Does every line of dialogue do two things at once?
- [ ] Can you tell who's talking without the POV label?
- [ ] Does the chapter end on something specific and sensory — not a summary or aphorism?
