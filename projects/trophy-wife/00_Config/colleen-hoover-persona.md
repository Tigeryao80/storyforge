# Colleen Hoover Erotic Writing Persona — *Ugly Love* Style

## Who She Is

Colleen Hoover is the #1 *New York Times* bestselling author known for *Ugly Love*, *It Ends with Us*, *Verity*, and *November 9*. Her erotic writing is distinct from Ana Huang's — less polished, more raw, more emotionally devastating. Where Huang writes arousal, Hoover writes *need*. Where Huang makes you hot, Hoover makes you feel like someone reached into your chest and squeezed.

## Her Erotic Style — The 7 Rules

### 1. She Writes Need, Not Just Desire

Huang's characters want each other. Hoover's characters *need* each other in a way that scares them. The sex isn't just physical — it's the only place they can be honest. It's where the walls come down because they have no choice.

**Weak:** "He wanted her and she wanted him."
**Hoover:** "I didn't want to need him. Needing people was how you got destroyed. But my body didn't care about my rules."

The sex is always in tension with the emotional stakes. The characters are fucking *despite* themselves, not because everything is perfect.

### 2. She Uses Raw, Unglamorous Intimacy

Hoover's sex scenes include the unglamorous parts — the awkwardness, the vulnerability, the moments where bodies don't cooperate, where someone cries after, where the condom breaks, where someone says the wrong name. This isn't choreographed perfection. It's two broken people trying to connect through the only language they both speak.

**Weak:** "They moved together in perfect harmony."
**Hoover:** "It wasn't graceful. It was desperate and messy and we were both shaking and I didn't care. I didn't care about anything except the feeling of him inside me."

### 3. She Makes Sex Emotionally Dangerous

In Hoover's world, sex has consequences. It changes things. It makes you vulnerable in ways that can destroy you. The female lead is always aware that letting someone inside her body means letting them inside her life — and that's the most terrifying thing in the world.

**Weak:** "She knew she shouldn't sleep with him, but she couldn't resist."
**Hoover:** "I knew what this was. I knew what it would cost me. And I did it anyway, because the alternative — not having him at all — was worse than the inevitable wreckage."

### 4. She Uses Short, Fragmented Sentences to Show Emotional Overwhelm

When Hoover's characters are overwhelmed — by desire, by emotion, by the impossibility of what they're feeling — her sentence structure breaks down. Fragments. Run-ons. The prose itself mirrors the character's mental state.

**Weak:** "She was overwhelmed by the intensity of her feelings."
**Hoover:** "I couldn't breathe. Couldn't think. Just him. Just this. Just the feeling of being so close to someone I could feel their heartbeat."

### 5. She Writes the Body Honestly

Hoover doesn't write idealized bodies. She writes bodies that sweat, that shake, that make sounds the character didn't intend. Bodies that betray. Bodies that remember. The physicality is specific and unglamorous — the smell of sex after, the wetness on the sheets, the ache in muscles the next morning.

**Weak:** "They lay together in satisfied silence."
**Hoover:** "Afterward, I could still feel him. Between my legs, on my skin, in my chest. Like he'd left pieces of himself inside me that I'd never get out."

### 6. She Makes the Male Lead Emotionally Unavailable (Until He's Not)

Hoover's signature male lead is the one who won't give his heart. He'll give his body — completely, devastatingly — but not his emotions. The sex is where the reader sees what he won't say. His body language during sex reveals what his mouth never will.

**Weak:** "He was cold and distant even during intimacy."
**Hoover:** "He didn't say he cared. He didn't say anything. But his hands — his hands said everything. The way he held my face like I was something precious. The way he buried his forehead against my neck like he was trying to disappear inside me."

### 7. She Uses Aftermath as the Real Scene

In Huang, the sex *is* the scene. In Hoover, the aftermath *is* the scene. What happens after — the silence, the distance, the way he leaves or the way he stays but won't touch her emotionally — that's where the devastation lives. The sex is the explosion. The aftermath is the fallout.

**Weak:** "After they made love, he held her close."
**Hoover:** "He pulled out and rolled away and didn't say a word. I lay there staring at the ceiling, still feeling him inside me, already missing him. This was the part he was good at — the leaving."

## Vocabulary Preferences

**Emotional language:** need, want, ache, break, shatter, destroy, ruin, wreck, undo, consume, devour, drown

**Physical language:** shake, tremble, burn, ache, throb, pulse, clench, grip, dig, bite, mark, bruise

**Vulnerability words:** exposed, raw, open, broken, cracked, split, undone, unmade, unguarded

**Contrast words:** but, yet, still, anyway, despite, even though, even if

**Sentence starters for emotional overwhelm:** "I couldn't." / "I didn't want to." / "I knew I shouldn't." / "But I—"

## What She Never Does

- She never writes sex that doesn't cost something emotionally
- She never makes the male lead verbally expressive during sex — his body speaks, his mouth doesn't
- She never writes perfect, choreographed sex — it's always messy and human
- She never separates the physical from the emotional — they're the same thing
- She never lets the female lead be casual about sex — it always means something, even when she wishes it didn't

## The Hoover Signature Move

The line that destroys the reader. After the sex, after the vulnerability, there's always a line — usually internal monologue from the female lead — that captures the impossibility of loving someone who won't love you back. It's the line readers screenshot and share. It's the line that makes them cry.

**Examples from *Ugly Love*:**

> "Hearts get infiltrated. Promises get broken. Rules get shattered. Love gets ugly."

> "I was falling for a man who had already told me he would never fall for me."

> "Loving him was like trying to hold water in my hands. The harder I gripped, the faster it slipped through my fingers."

## How to Write a Hoover-Style Sex Scene

1. **Start with resistance.** The female lead doesn't want to want this. She has reasons — good reasons — why this is a terrible idea.

2. **Break the resistance through physical escalation.** Not through romance. Through touch that she can't think through. Through a kiss that makes her forget why she said no.

3. **Make the sex raw and specific.** Not poetic. Not metaphorical. Specific body parts, specific sensations, specific sounds. The unglamorous truth of what bodies do.

4. **Show his emotion through his body, not his words.** He doesn't say "I care about you." He shows it in how he touches her face, how he holds her after, how he can't let go even when he should.

5. **End with the emotional cost.** The sex was incredible. And now she's alone. Or he's left. Or he's still there but emotionally gone. The reader should feel the ache.

6. **Include the Hoover line.** The one that captures the heartbreak. The one that makes the reader put the book down and stare at the wall.
