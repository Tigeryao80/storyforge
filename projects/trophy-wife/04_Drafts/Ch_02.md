# Chapter 2: The Morning After

She was still asleep.

Alexander Voss stood in the doorway of the master suite and watched his wife breathe. He'd been standing there for eleven minutes. He knew because he'd checked his watch at 6:11 and it was now 6:22 and she hadn't moved except for the small rise and fall of her ribs under the sheet.

He should make breakfast. That was what husbands did. Husbands who hadn't spent the previous night revealing they knew their wife was a federal agent, anyway.

He turned from the doorway and walked downstairs.

The kitchen was white marble and steel, designed by a woman in Copenhagen who'd charged him four hundred thousand dollars to make it look like no one ever cooked here. He pulled eggs from the refrigerator. Bread from the proofing drawer. Butter that cost more per ounce than the TriBeCa apartment he'd bought just to watch her sleep. He cracked six eggs into a bowl — two for her, four for him, though he wouldn't eat four. He'd eat two and leave two because leaving food on a plate was a kind of honesty.

Six months ago, he'd known her name.

Not her real name — that came later. First it was a face on a surveillance feed, walking into a charity gala at the Metropolitan Museum like she belonged there. Black dress. Dark hair. Green eyes behind clear contacts that were meant to look like nothing. She'd scanned the room the way people scan rooms when they're looking for exits, not friends.

He'd had Marcus pull her identity by morning. Vivian Cross. FBI — financial crimes with a trafficking sideline, a specialist in the very thing that had taken her mother. Twenty-nine. Foster care. The pattern was right there in her file, and the Bureau had been too stupid to see it.

He'd read the file three times. Then he'd read it again.

Then he'd started watching.

Not through the security feeds — that came later, after the penthouse, after he'd bought the apartment across the hall from hers. After he'd realized he couldn't stop. He knew her schedule before she did. The runs. The coffee. The 2 AM meetings in a Queens parking garage she thought no one saw.

He watched her the way he watched the market — constantly, with no intention of stopping, and no idea when he'd lost control of it.

The eggs went into the pan. Low heat. He stirred them slowly, patiently, removing them before they looked done because they kept cooking after. Control was knowing when to stop.

She'd been in the shower for four minutes. He could hear the water from the kitchen — the estate's plumbing was copper, it carried sound — and he was calculating how long she'd been awake. Long enough to think. Long enough to run through her options. Long enough to remember the gun in her suitcase.

She wouldn't use it. He was almost certain. Almost.

The toast popped. He buttered it and set it on a plate next to the eggs and carried the tray upstairs. His hands were steady. They were always steady. That was the point of them.

He found her sitting on the edge of the bed in his shirt. Just the shirt. Her hair was wet. She'd already been through his closet.

"The study," she said. No greeting. "I need to see it."

He set the tray on the nightstand. "Breakfast first."

"I'm not hungry."

"You're always hungry. You just don't eat when you're thinking." He pulled the chair from the corner and sat down across from her. "Eat. Then I'll show you whatever you want to see."

She looked at him. The green eyes were doing the thing they did — cataloging, assessing, running probabilities. He'd watched her do it for six months through cameras and now she was doing it three feet away, deciding whether the eggs were more useful than an argument.

She took the plate. Ate standing up. Didn't sit in the chair. That was fine. He'd expected it.

"You knew," she said, not looking at him. "Six months."

"Yes."

"You knew when you proposed."

"Yes."

"You knew when you sent the dress."

"Yes."

She set the plate down. Half the eggs gone. More than he'd expected. "Why."

It wasn't *why did you marry me*. It was *why are you telling me you knew*. The distinction mattered.

"Because I'm tired of pretending," he said. "And because you're going to find out anyway. Better you hear it from me than discover it."

"Discover what."

He stood. Crossed to the window. The Atlantic was grey this morning — it matched the sky, which matched the marble, which matched his eyes. He noticed these things. He didn't know why.

He told her about Catherine. Her face didn't change. But her hand — the small unconscious movement toward her wrist — told him everything.

"The people who took her," he said, "are part of a network. I've been tracking that network for fifteen years. Since they took my sister."

"Your sister is Elena Voss."

"You've done your homework."

"You've made it easy. Your sister's case is public record."

"Elena was sixteen. They held her for four months. I found her by accident — a shipping container in Newark, wrong port, wrong manifest, a mistake that saved her life." He turned from the window. "The people who took her are still operating. They've been operating for thirty years. They move women across borders using my company's logistics network — or they did, until I started dismantling it from the inside."

She was watching him the way she'd watched him at the gala. Looking for the lie.

"You're telling me you're not the trafficker."

"I'm telling you I'm the reason the trafficker is losing money."

"The Bureau says—"

"The Bureau says a lot of things. The Bureau sent you here to confirm what they already believe because confirming is easier than investigating." He sat back down. "I'm not asking you to trust me, Vivian. I'm asking you to look at the evidence and decide for yourself."

"The evidence in your study."

"Among other things."

She picked up the plate again. Finished the eggs this time. Set it down empty. "Show me."

He stood and walked to the painting on the far wall — a Rothko, red on black, worth eleven million dollars and completely hideous. He'd bought it because it was the perfect size to cover a wall safe. Behind the safe: three hard drives, two laptops, and forty-seven names.

He moved the painting. Opened the safe. Pulled out the first hard drive and handed it to her.

"Everything I have," he said. "Financial records. Shipping manifests. Communications. Names of buyers, sellers, facilitators. Five years of work."

She took the drive. Held it like it might explode.

"There's a laptop on the desk," he said. "Password is Elena's birthday. You know it already — it's in my file."

"You know I've read your file."

"I know you've read the file the Bureau gave you. That's not the same thing."

She looked at him. And for a moment — just a moment — the mask slipped and he saw what was underneath. Not fear. Not anger. Exhaustion. The kind that comes from carrying something for so long you forget what it feels like to put it down.

"I need to make a call," she said.

"Your earpiece is in your clutch. The mic in the lamp is still active. Your handler has heard everything since I opened the bedroom door." He paused. "I didn't sweep the room. I wanted him to hear."

Her jaw tightened. "You wanted MERIDIAN to know you know."

"I wanted MERIDIAN to know the game has changed."

She stared at him. He let her. He was good at being stared at — fifteen years of boardrooms and interrogations and nights like last night had made him fluent in silence.

"What do you want from me?" she asked. Same question as last night. Different weight.

"Same thing I said before. The truth. Just once."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one I have."

She turned and walked to the bathroom. He heard the clutch open. Heard her mutter something — prayer or curse — and with Vivian Cross, he suspected the difference was academic.

He sat in the chair and looked at the bed. The sheets were still tangled from last night. Her robe was on the floor where it had fallen. The room smelled like her — underneath the roses, underneath his cologne, there was something that was just *her*, and he'd been smelling it on surveillance footage for six months through cameras that couldn't capture scent, which meant his brain had invented it, which meant he was more compromised than he'd thought.

She came back out. Earpiece in. Face reset.

"I need to go for a walk," she said.

"Take your time."

"I'll be an hour."

"Take two."

She left. He heard her footsteps on the stairs, heard the front door open and close, heard the gravel under her shoes as she walked toward the cliffs.

He picked up the breakfast tray. Carried it downstairs. Washed the plate. Dried it. Put it back. Then he washed the carving knife he hadn't used and put it back too.

Then he went to his study, sat at the desk, and opened the security feed on his laptop. She was on the cliff path, walking fast, one hand pressed to her ear. Talking to MERIDIAN. He couldn't hear the conversation — the cameras on the path had no audio, he'd have to fix that — but he could see her face.

She looked like a woman trying to decide which direction to run.

He watched her for six minutes. Then he closed the laptop. Forty-seven names. He'd crossed off three. Forty-four left. One of them was MERIDIAN. He was almost certain. Almost.

Almost wasn't enough to act on. Only enough to keep watching.

And Alexander Voss was very good at watching.
